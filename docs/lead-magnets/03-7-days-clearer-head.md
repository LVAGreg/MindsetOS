### EMAIL 1 — Day 1

**Subject:** You're not undisciplined. You're just stuck in a loop.
**Preview text:** The real reason your brain keeps recycling the same three thoughts.

---

Hey —

Welcome to 7 Days to a Clearer Head. I'm Greg.

Quick housekeeping: you're going to get one email from me every morning for the next seven days. Each one is short. Each one has one idea and one tiny thing to try. That's it.

No workbook. No Facebook group. No "accountability partner" you ghost after Day 2.

Just you and your inbox.

Here's where we start.

---

**The thing nobody tells you about "thinking in circles."**

When you catch yourself replaying the same worry for the third time before lunch, the instinct is to blame yourself.

*I should be more focused.*
*I should be better at this by now.*
*Why can't I just decide and move on?*

But here's what's actually happening: your brain isn't broken. It's doing exactly what it's designed to do.

Your mind is a pattern-completion machine. Its job — its *entire* job — is to find open loops and try to close them. That's why you can't stop thinking about the email you haven't replied to, or the hire you're not sure about, or that thing your business partner said on Tuesday.

Those aren't distractions. They're open loops. And your brain will keep circling back to them because that's what brains do with unfinished business.

The problem isn't that you think in circles.

The problem is that you think the circles mean something is wrong with you.

They don't. They mean you're human, you're running a business, and you have a lot of open loops.

That reframe matters more than it sounds. Because the moment you stop fighting the loops and start *seeing* them for what they are — your brain doing its job, not evidence of your failure — something loosens.

Not everything. But enough to notice.

---

**Before tomorrow:**

At some point today, you'll catch yourself in a loop. Same thought, second or third pass. When you notice it, don't try to stop it. Just name it.

"Ah. That's the hiring loop again."
"There's the money loop."
"That's the one about the conversation I'm avoiding."

Name it. That's all. See what happens when you do.

Talk tomorrow.

— Greg

### EMAIL 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** The you that's reading this isn't the you that's stressed.
**Preview text:** There's a part of you that can watch your own thinking. That changes everything.

---

Hey —

Yesterday I asked you to notice a thought loop and name it.

If you did it — even once — you just demonstrated something most people never realize they can do.

You *watched yourself think.*

Which means there are two things happening at once: the thinking, and the watching.

---

**The Observer vs. The Actor.**

Here's the simplest way I can put this.

There's a version of you that's *in* the situation. Stressed about the launch. Annoyed at the client. Worried about payroll. Replaying what you should've said in that meeting.

That's The Actor. The one on stage, in the scene, reacting in real time.

Then there's the version of you that can step back and go: "Huh. I've been anxious about this for forty-five minutes and I haven't actually done anything about it."

That's The Observer.

The Observer doesn't fix anything. It doesn't need to. Its only job is to *see what's happening* without being consumed by it.

This is not meditation. I'm not going to ask you to sit on a cushion and watch your breath. This is a practical skill — the ability to notice what your mind is doing while your mind is doing it.

And it's a skill that changes how you run your business.

Because most of the bad decisions entrepreneurs make don't come from a lack of information. They come from being so deep inside The Actor that they can't see the scene clearly.

You fire the person because you're frustrated, not because it's the right call.
You say yes to the project because you're anxious about revenue, not because it's a fit.
You avoid the conversation because The Actor is scared, even though The Observer knows it needs to happen.

The shift isn't about eliminating The Actor. You need The Actor — that's the part of you that *does* things.

The shift is about giving The Observer a seat at the table.

---

**Before tomorrow:**

Today, pick one moment — a meeting, a decision, a conversation — and see if you can activate The Observer for thirty seconds.

You'll know it's working because you'll feel a tiny gap between what's happening and your reaction to it. Like you're watching a movie you're also starring in.

It might only last a few seconds. That's fine. A few seconds is plenty.

— Greg

### EMAIL 3 — Day 3

**Subject:** Your business probably runs on three thoughts. Here they are.
**Preview text:** Most entrepreneurs cycle between the same 3 mental patterns all day.

---

Hey —

Something I noticed when I started paying attention to my own thinking — really paying attention, not just "being mindful" in the abstract — is that I wasn't actually thinking thousands of different thoughts per day.

I was thinking the same three. On repeat. With slight variations.

---

**The 3 thoughts that run most businesses.**

Every entrepreneur I've worked with — and this held true for me too — has a small set of recurring thought patterns that drive about 80% of their decisions, reactions, and stress.

They tend to fall into three categories:

**1. The Identity Thought**
"Am I good enough for this?"

This one wears a lot of costumes. Sometimes it looks like impostor syndrome. Sometimes it sounds like "I should be further along." Sometimes it shows up as over-preparing, over-delivering, or over-explaining — because deep down, you're trying to prove something to someone. Maybe yourself.

**2. The Control Thought**
"If I don't handle this, it'll fall apart."

This is the one that keeps you in the weeds. Checking other people's work. Redoing things yourself. Saying "it's just faster if I do it." It's not a work ethic problem — it's a thought pattern that says the business can't survive your absence.

**3. The Scarcity Thought**
"What if this stops working?"

Revenue is up? "But what about next quarter." Client is happy? "But what if they leave." Things are going well? "This can't last." The scarcity thought never takes a day off. It just shifts what it's worried about.

---

Here's the thing: these aren't character flaws. They're mental habits. And they feel invisible because you've been running them so long they just feel like... you.

But they're not you. They're patterns. And patterns can be seen, named, and — eventually — interrupted.

You don't need to fix them today. You just need to know they exist.

Because once you see the pattern, it loses about half its power. Not because you've done anything heroic. Just because awareness is surprisingly disruptive to autopilot.

---

**Before tomorrow:**

Which of the three do you default to most? Identity, Control, or Scarcity?

You probably already know. You felt it as you read the descriptions.

Just sit with that for a minute today. Notice when it shows up. You don't need to change it. Just notice it with the same energy you'd notice the weather.

"Oh, there's the Control Thought again. Interesting."

That's it.

— Greg

### EMAIL 4 — Day 4

**Subject:** There's a gap between what happens and what you do about it. It's about 2 seconds long.
**Preview text:** The most useful skill nobody teaches entrepreneurs.

---

Hey —

Yesterday I asked you to spot your dominant thought pattern. Identity, Control, or Scarcity.

If you spotted it even once, you just did something most people never do — you caught the pattern *while it was running.*

Today we're going to talk about what to do in that moment. Because that moment — the one between the trigger and your reaction — is where everything changes.

---

**The Gap.**

There's a space between something happening and you responding to it. It's small. Maybe two seconds. Sometimes less.

A client sends a frustrating email. A team member misses a deadline. Your revenue dips for a week. Someone questions your decision in a meeting.

In that space — before you fire back the reply, before you take over the task, before you spiral into "what if this all falls apart" — there's a gap.

Most of the time, you blow right past it. Trigger → Reaction. So fast it feels like one thing.

But it's not one thing. It's two things with a space between them.

And that space? That's where you get to choose.

Not choose your feelings — you feel what you feel. But choose what you *do* with the feeling.

The Gap is where The Observer lives. It's the moment where you can catch The Actor mid-scene and ask: "Is this the move? Or is this the pattern?"

I'll be honest — I spent years not knowing this gap existed. I thought my reactions *were* my decisions. It wasn't until I started treating my own thinking as something I could watch — not just something that happened to me — that I realized I had a choice I'd been skipping.

Finding The Gap doesn't mean you become slow or passive. It means you become *deliberate.* And deliberate beats reactive every single time in business.

---

**How to find it:**

You can't manufacture The Gap. But you can start noticing it.

The next time you feel a strong reaction — frustration, anxiety, the urge to jump in and fix something — pause. One breath. That's not a meditation technique. It's just enough time for The Observer to wake up.

One breath. Then decide.

Sometimes you'll still do the same thing you would've done anyway. Fine. But you'll have *chosen* it instead of been *pulled* into it. That's a completely different operating system.

---

**Before tomorrow:**

Find The Gap once today. Just once.

Something will trigger you — it always does. When it happens, take one breath before you respond. See what you notice in that one-breath pause.

It might feel like nothing. Do it anyway.

— Greg

### EMAIL 5 — Day 5

**Subject:** Clarity doesn't feel like you think it does.
**Preview text:** It's quieter than confidence. And way more useful.

---

Hey —

We're five days in. Let's talk about the thing you're actually after.

Clarity.

Everybody wants it. But almost nobody can describe what it actually feels like — because we've confused it with something else.

---

**What clarity is not.**

Clarity is not certainty. Certainty is knowing the outcome before you start. Clarity is knowing the *next move* without needing to know the outcome.

Clarity is not confidence. Confidence is a feeling. Clarity is a state. You can feel deeply unsure and still be clear about what needs to happen next.

Clarity is not motivation. Motivation is energy. Clarity is direction. You can be exhausted and still be clear.

Here's what I think clarity actually is:

**Clarity is the absence of noise around a decision.**

Not the absence of difficulty. Not the absence of risk. Just... the noise drops away and what's left is the thing.

The hire you need to make.
The offer you need to change.
The conversation you've been avoiding.

You've felt it before. Probably in a shower, or on a walk, or at 11pm when you weren't trying to think about work. A thought drops in — clean, quiet, obvious — and you wonder why it took you so long to see it.

That wasn't luck. That was your brain finally getting enough space to do what it's good at.

---

**The trap entrepreneurs fall into.**

We think clarity comes from *more thinking.* More analysis. More pros-and-cons lists. More "strategic planning sessions" that are really just organized worrying.

But clarity doesn't come from adding. It comes from subtracting.

When you named your thought loops on Day 1, you subtracted noise.
When you activated The Observer on Day 2, you subtracted reactivity.
When you identified your dominant pattern on Day 3, you subtracted confusion.
When you found The Gap on Day 4, you subtracted autopilot.

You weren't adding new skills. You were removing interference.

That's the whole game. Not building a better mind. Removing what's in the way of the one you've already got.

---

**Before tomorrow:**

Notice one moment today where you feel clear. Even a small one. A decision that feels clean. A conversation where you said exactly what you meant. A moment where the noise just... wasn't there.

Don't chase it. Just notice it when it shows up. And pay attention to what the conditions were. What were you doing? What were you *not* doing?

Clarity leaves clues. Start collecting them.

— Greg

### EMAIL 6 — Day 6

**Subject:** The one question that cuts through almost any mental fog.
**Preview text:** I use this one almost daily. It's embarrassingly simple.

---

Hey —

I promised you one question that cuts through mental fog. Here it is.

---

**"What am I actually afraid of here?"**

That's it.

I know. It sounds too simple. Maybe even a little therapy-adjacent. But stay with me.

When I'm stuck — really stuck, the kind where I've been "thinking about" something for three days without making a move — it's almost never because I don't know what to do.

It's because I'm afraid of something I haven't named yet.

Afraid the launch will flop and I'll look foolish.
Afraid I'll have the conversation and the relationship won't survive it.
Afraid that if I commit to this direction, I'm closing a door I can't reopen.
Afraid that I'm not actually as good at this as people think.

The unnamed fear is what creates the fog. Not the complexity of the situation. The situation is usually pretty straightforward once the fear is out of the way.

---

**Why this works.**

Unnamed fear behaves like background radiation. It's everywhere, influencing everything, and you can't see it. Your brain processes it as "I need to think about this more" — which is just code for "I'm avoiding something and I don't want to look at it directly."

When you name the fear, something shifts. Not because the fear goes away — sometimes it doesn't. But because a named fear is a *manageable* fear. It's specific. It's contained. You can look at it and ask: "Okay. If that happened... then what?"

And "then what" almost always has an answer that isn't catastrophic.

I use this question probably three or four times a week. In the shower. Driving. Sitting at my desk staring at a task I've been avoiding. And nine times out of ten, within a minute of answering honestly, the fog clears and the next step is obvious.

Not comfortable. Not easy. But obvious.

---

**Before tomorrow:**

You've got something you've been circling. Something you've been "thinking about" but haven't moved on. You know exactly what it is — it just popped into your head.

Ask yourself: "What am I actually afraid of here?"

Answer honestly. Out loud if you can. Write it down if you want. But at minimum, let the answer form without editing it.

See what happens to the fog.

— Greg
